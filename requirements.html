Section 1 - Project Overview​

Section 1.1 - Project Background​

MotionInput is a software package that enables touchless computing and gaming on a wide range of computers using only the built-in webcam and microphone. It replaces the functionality of the keyboard and mouse with body gestures and speech commands, allowing users to navigate and interact with their computers without physical contact.

The primary objective of MotionInput is to enhance accessibility and make computing more inclusive. By facilitating touchless interaction, it aims to empower users with disabilities, enabling them to navigate the web and play games with greater ease. MotionInput strives to bring the benefits of touchless computing to as many hardware configurations as possible, making it a versatile solution for a diverse range of users.

Section 1.2 - Our Project Goal​

Efficiency, Integration, and Error Correction

Our team had a series of complex goals that stretched across various projects that comprises MotionInput. These goals focused on three areas: Efficiency, Error Correction, and Integration:

Efficiency: Investigate the current performance of MotionInput modes and improve the efficiency of MotionInput where it matters. Focus specifically on modes that need these performance improvements to run on lower-end hardware. This involved optimising system components to ensure smoother and faster operations, resulting in a more responsive and seamless user experience. We also aim to maintain high FPS performance for a spectrum of device specifications (low-end and high-end Windows devices).

Error Correction: Understand and investigate the current limitations and errors encountered by users and developers when using MotionInput. Our aim was to develop mitigations and solutions to address these issues, thereby enhancing the reliability and robustness of MotionInput.

Integration: The third goal was to integrate various modes (gestures and other configurations) to cater to different use cases. This meant our team would be working closely with multiple teams across various parts of the project, rather than focusing on a single application.

In essence, our mission entailed fine-tuning MotionInput to minimise errors, maximise efficiency, and ensure seamless integration of different modes and configurations. To achieve these goals, crafting user requirements that address the needs and challenges of diverse user groups was crucial.

Section 2 - Key Target Audiences​

Due to the nature of our project, we worked with a wide variety of MotionInput teams on many distinct parts of MotionInput with very different audiences. We’ve detailed numerous key target audiences below as each was relevant to a specific goal or part of our project.

How we gathered requirements for clients

To comprehensively understand the requirements discussed in this section, we conducted preliminary research and engaged in meetings with our clients. More specifically, we conducted user testing and interviews with teachers and students (under the supervision of teachers and educational therapists) at the Richard Cloudesley School. Through these invaluable interactions, we identified and formulated several key requirements. These requirements have been thoroughly discussed and mutually agreed upon with our clients, ensuring alignment with their needs and expectations.

Section 2.1 - MotionInput for Education: Teachers and Students​

  • Reliable Gesture Recognition: Ensure consistent recognition of teachers' gestures from various distances, potentially even with students in the camera's field of view, to minimise interruptions during teaching.
  • Customisability: Provide adjustable settings to accommodate students with diverse needs, including learning difficulties and physical limitations, such as one-handed control for navigation and inking.
  • PowerPoint Integration: Enable seamless hand navigation and inking capabilities within MS PowerPoint, allowing teachers to navigate slides, annotate, and highlight key points using gestures.
  • Speech-To-Text Transcription: Incorporate real-time speech-to-text transcription to support students with note-taking and assist those with hearing or auditory processing difficulties.
  • Interactive Learning Tools: Develop hand navigation-based 3D tools for subjects like geography and object manipulation to enhance student engagement and understanding.

Section 2.2 - Retirees​

  • Intuitive User Interface: Design an intuitive and easily navigable interface with clear visual cues, large icons, and simple menus to cater to users who may not be tech-savvy.
  • Customisable Error Tolerance: Offer sensitivity settings to minimise unintended inputs caused by tremors or unsteady hands, ensuring accurate gesture recognition.
  • Hand Navigation for Daily Tasks: Incorporate hand navigation capabilities into common daily tasks, such as web browsing and file management, to simplify computer usage for retirees.
  • Accessibility Features: Include speech-to-text functionality to facilitate text input and dictation, reducing the need for manual typing.
  • Intuitive Gesture Learning: Ensure that gestures are intuitive and extensively tested to reduce the minimise the learning curve for retirees and provide a personalised experience tailored to their specific abilities and preferences.

Section 2.3 - Children with Special Physical Needs​

  • Flexible Input Methods: Ensure the system can recognise a wide range of movements and gestures, accommodating children with varying motor skills and control, including subtle facial expressions and head movements.
  • Face Navigation: Ensure robust face navigation capabilities allow children to control devices using facial expressions and head movements, benefiting those with physical disabilities or limited hand dexterity.
  • Customisable Gesture Recognition: Provide alternative triggers that recognise click actions without fully closing the hand (for users unable to do this) or using different finger pinch gestures to accommodate individual abilities.
  • Engaging User Interface: Design an intuitive and engaging interface that ensures children are not intimidated by assistive technology through interactive elements and visual cues.

Section 2.4 - Amputees​

  • Body Point Tracking: Develop limb tracking functionality to enable individuals with missing limbs to navigate and play games by moving other limbs, catering to those with severe mobility limitations.

Section 2.5 - General User Requirements​

  • Compatibility: Ensure compatibility with a wide range of hardware configurations, including low-end devices, to maximise accessibility without requiring users to upgrade their systems.
  • Developer Support: Provide comprehensive development tools and documentation to support developers in creating new modes and integrating them into the system with minimal errors, streamlining the development process.

Section 4 - MoSCoW Requirements​

Section 4.1 - MoSCoW Requirements (Functional)​

Must Have:

  • Identify and resolve the most common failure users experience with key migrated features to ensure a stable and reliable user experience.
  • Implement double pinch gesture and create a new RepeatedPose architecture to support the creation of repeated poses, enhancing the flexibility of the system.
  • Migrate face navigation capabilities, improve robustness and develop an overlay system to provide users with visual feedback and guidance while using face-based controls.
  • Introduce five-finger navigation for inking to offer users more precise and intuitive control over digital ink and drawing.
  • Enhance inking functionality by improving accuracy, depth sensitivity, and removing the need for calibration, resulting in a more seamless and efficient inking experience.
  • Enable configurable sensitivity settings for each finger during pinching gestures to accommodate individual user preferences and capabilities.
  • Incorporate a sensitivity variable for hand navigation to allow users to adjust the responsiveness of the system based on their needs and comfort level.
  • Integrate essential mouse modules such as double-click, scroll, zoom in/out to provide users with a comprehensive set of navigation options.
  • Expand the keyboard module to support trackpad functionality and multiple key presses, increasing the versatility of the text input experience.
  • Introduce new hand poses to broaden the range of available gestures and provide users with more options for interaction and control.
  • Implement a zoom event for hand navigation to enable users to easily magnify content and focus on specific areas of the screen.
  • Develop a Dual-Hand navigation mode to support advanced users who prefer using both hands for more efficient navigation and control.

Should Have:

  • Conduct performance profiling on MotionInput v3.4 to evaluate and document the performance of different features across various systems.
  • Enhance speech model accuracy for children by integrating OpenAI Whisper model support, catering to a wider range of users.
  • Optimise gesture recognition and creation applications to streamline the development of modes and features.
  • Develop a new MFC for MotionInput Face Navigation and seamlessly integrate it with the backend.
  • Implement a "raise hand to transcribe" feature for the education mode, ensuring minimal false triggers in a classroom environment.
  • Migrate forcefields and enhance accuracy by introducing a buffer system.
  • Support multiple trigger gestures to provide users with increased flexibility and control.
  • Develop a BodyPoint Joystick mode that utilises facial navigation logic for arbitrary body points.
  • Enable 3D object rotation functionality within the hand navigation module.

Could Have:

  • Enhance MotionInput tracking performance in multi-user environments, such as classrooms, to minimise interference from background users.
  • Collaborate with the Eye Gaze team to resolve multithreading issues in their eye tracking assistive technology.
  • Implement a real-time mode to minimise jitter in performance-sensitive modes.
  • Develop a system for creating complex overlays by composing multiple smaller overlays, enabling more sophisticated user interfaces.
  • Apply image blur to the background using object detection techniques (e.g., YOLO classifier and Camshift algorithm) to prioritize foreground user detection.
  • Optimise gesture recognition by converting unnecessary Python files into JSON format.

Won’t Have:

  • Streamline the compilation process and improve compilation testing for MotionInput.

Section 4.2 - MoSCoW requirements (Non-Functional)​

Must Have:

  • Optimise MotionInput v3.4 to ensure efficient performance on dual-core and low-end systems.
  • Minimise errors and inaccuracies in common user interactions with MotionInput.
  • Maintain well-organised and documented codebase to facilitate updates and troubleshooting.
  • Support the migration to MotionInput v3.4 and optimise the development process.
  • Ensure developed software is backward compatible with Windows XP machines for use by the NHS.

Should Have:

  • Enable the creation of complex overlays by combining multiple components, enhancing UI richness.
  • Modularise MotionInput features (modes) to facilitate thorough testing and defect identification.
  • Ensure seamless integration of MFCs with the new MotionInput v3.4 backend.

Could Have:

  • Develop new gesture recognition features that cater to diverse physical abilities.
  • Ensure compatibility with a wide range of hardware and software platforms for seamless operation.
  • Design a modular architecture that allows for easy addition of new features and modes.
  • Promote cross-platform compatibility, enabling use across different operating systems and environments.

Won't Have:

  • Implement adaptability to different languages and regions.
